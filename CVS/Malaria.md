# Malaria

*(Exam Note: The Bhalani syllabus heavily emphasizes the Life Cycle of Plasmodium, Morphology of P. vivax, Complications of P. falciparum/Malignant Tertian Malaria, and an exhaustive Laboratory Diagnosis)* [1, 2].

## 1. Definition & Etiological Agent
*   **Definition:** Malaria is a mosquito-borne infectious disease caused by protozoan parasites of the genus *Plasmodium* [3].
*   **Causative Species:**
    *   ***P. falciparum*:** Causes malignant tertian malaria (most severe) [3].
    *   ***P. vivax*:** Causes benign tertian malaria [3].
    *   ***P. malariae*:** Causes benign quartan malaria [3].
    *   ***P. ovale*:** Causes ovale tertian malaria [3].
    *   ***P. knowlesi*:** Causes quotidian (simian) malaria [3].

## 2. Life Cycle
*   **Hosts:** *Plasmodium* completes its life cycle in two hosts: the female *Anopheles* mosquito acts as the definitive host (sexual cycle), and man acts as the intermediate host (asexual cycle) [4].
*   **Infective Form:** Sporozoites, transmitted to humans via mosquito bite [4].
*   **Human Cycle (Asexual - Schizogony):**
    1.  **Pre-erythrocytic Schizogony:** Sporozoites infect hepatocytes, transforming into trophozoites and then schizonts, which rupture to release hepatic merozoites [4]. *(Note: P. vivax and P. ovale form dormant "hypnozoites" in the liver, causing relapses years later)* [4].
    2.  **Erythrocytic Schizogony:** Merozoites infect RBCs, developing into ring forms $\rightarrow$ trophozoites $\rightarrow$ schizonts [4, 5]. RBC rupture releases more merozoites, causing the classic febrile paroxysms [6].
    3.  **Gametogony:** Some merozoites differentiate into sexual forms (male microgametocytes and female macrogametocytes) which are ingested by mosquitoes during a blood meal [5].

## 3. Pathogenesis & Complications (Malignant Tertian Malaria)
*   **Pathogenesis of *P. falciparum*:** Exhibits "cytoadherence" mediated by *P. falciparum* erythrocyte membrane protein-1 (PfEMP-1) [6]. This allows infected RBCs to sequester in deep visceral capillaries, avoiding splenic clearance [6, 7]. Thus, only ring forms and gametocytes are seen in peripheral blood, not late trophozoites or schizonts [5].
*   **Complications of *P. falciparum*:**
    *   **Cerebral Malaria:** The most serious complication, caused by capillary plugging and vascular occlusion leading to cerebral anoxia [7].
    *   **Pernicious Malaria:** Manifests as Algid malaria (profound shock/hypotension) or Septicemic malaria [7].
    *   **Blackwater Fever:** Sudden intravascular hemolysis, hemoglobinuria (dark urine), and fever, often following quinine therapy [7].

## 4. Laboratory Diagnosis (Exhaustive Coverage)
*Specimen Collection: Peripheral blood is collected from the ear lobe or finger prick, ideally a few hours after the fever peak and before antimalarials are given* [8].

**A. Microscopic Tests (Gold Standard)**
*   **Thick Smear:** A big drop of blood spread thickly [8]. It is 40 times more sensitive than a thin smear and can detect 5-10 parasites/µL, making it ideal for *detecting* the parasite and quantifying the load [9].
*   **Thin Smear:** A small drop of blood spread with a feathery tail [8]. It is used specifically for *speciation* based on parasite morphology and RBC changes [9].
    *   ***P. vivax* Morphology:** Enlarged RBCs, amoeboid late trophozoites, large schizonts (12-24 merozoites), and round gametocytes [9, 10].
    *   ***P. falciparum* Morphology:** Normal RBC size [9]. Exhibits multiple ring forms, accole/applique forms (rings attached to the RBC membrane), and double dot (headphone-shaped) rings [9, 10]. The gametocytes are characteristically **crescent or banana-shaped** [10]. Late trophozoites and schizonts are absent in peripheral blood [10].

**B. Quantitative Buffy Coat (QBC) Examination**
*   **Principle:** Blood is collected in a capillary tube coated with acridine orange and centrifuged [11]. The buffy coat is examined under a UV light source [11].
*   **Result:** Parasitized RBCs appear as brilliant green dots [11].
*   **Advantages & Disadvantages:** It is faster, more sensitive, and allows quantification, but it is expensive and speciation is difficult [11].

**C. Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs / ICTs)**
*   **Principle:** Immunochromatographic tests that detect specific malarial antigens using a nitrocellulose membrane [11].
*   **Antigens Detected:**
    *   **HRP-II (Histidine-rich protein-2):** Specific to *P. falciparum* only [11].
    *   **LDH and Aldolase:** Pan-malarial antigens produced by all *Plasmodium* species [11].
*   **Interpretation:** Test kits have specific lines (T1 and T2) that allow differentiation between a *P. falciparum* infection and non-falciparum infections based on which colored bands appear [11, 12].

**D. Culture**
*   **Method:** Trager and Jensen's method utilizing RPMI 1640 medium [12].
*   **Use:** Rarely used for primary diagnosis; it is primarily utilized for research and the preparation of malaria antigens [12].

**E. Molecular Methods**
*   **Nested Multiplex PCR:** Targets 18S rDNA for highly accurate speciation [12].
*   **Real-time PCR:** Useful for precise quantification and detection of drug-resistance genes [12].

## 5. Treatment & Prophylaxis
*   **Treatment of *P. vivax*:** Chloroquine plus Primaquine (Primaquine is mandatory for 14 days to kill liver hypnozoites and prevent relapse) [12, 13].
*   **Treatment of *P. falciparum*:** Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT), such as Artemether-lumefantrine, is recommended [13].
*   **Chemoprophylaxis:** Doxycycline is recommended for short-term travelers (<6 weeks), while Mefloquine is used for long-term prophylaxis [13].
*   **Vaccination:** RTS, S/AS01 is the only approved vaccine, containing the pre-erythrocytic circumsporozoite protein (Pf-CSP) of *P. falciparum* [14].
