# **OBSTETRICS & GYNAECOLOGY – LONG ANSWER QUESTION 1 (15 Marks)**

## **Question 1. Enumerate the types of abortions and discuss the management of Threatened Abortion.**

**(Standard References: DC Dutta's Textbook of Obstetrics, Williams Obstetrics)**

---

# Definition

**Abortion** is the **expulsion or extraction of the products of conception before the fetus reaches viability**, i.e., **before 20 weeks of gestation or fetal weight less than 500 g** 

---

# Classification (Types) of Abortion

## A. Spontaneous Abortion

Occurs naturally without any medical intervention.

### 1. Threatened abortion

* Vaginal bleeding before 20 weeks
* Cervix closed
* Fetus alive

### 2. Inevitable abortion

* Bleeding with pain
* Cervix dilated
* Abortion cannot be prevented

### 3. Incomplete abortion

* Some products expelled
* Some retained in uterus

### 4. Complete abortion

* All products expelled
* Uterus empty
* Cervix closes

### 5. Missed abortion

* Fetal death
* Products retained inside uterus

### 6. Septic abortion

* Abortion associated with uterine infection

### 7. Recurrent (Habitual) abortion

* **Three or more consecutive spontaneous abortions** before viability.

---

## B. Induced Abortion

Termination of pregnancy by medical or surgical methods.

### i. Therapeutic abortion

Performed for medical indications under the **Medical Termination of Pregnancy (MTP) Act**.

### ii. Elective abortion

Termination at the request of the woman within legal limits.

---

# Flowchart

```text
Abortion
      │
 ┌────┴─────┐
 │          │
Spontaneous  Induced
 │            │
 ├ Threatened
 ├ Inevitable
 ├ Incomplete
 ├ Complete
 ├ Missed
 ├ Septic
 └ Recurrent
```

---

# Threatened Abortion

## Definition

Threatened abortion is **vaginal bleeding occurring before 20 weeks of gestation with a closed cervical os and a live fetus**.

---

# Etiology (Causes)

### Maternal causes

* Trauma
* Heavy physical exertion
* Progesterone deficiency
* Uterine anomalies
* Fibroids
* Infections
* Endocrine disorders

  * Diabetes mellitus
  * Thyroid disorders

### Fetal causes

* Chromosomal abnormalities
* Congenital malformations

### Placental causes

* Subchorionic hematoma

---

# Clinical Features

### Symptoms

* Mild vaginal bleeding
* Spotting
* Mild lower abdominal pain
* Backache

### Signs

* General condition stable
* Cervix closed
* Uterus corresponds to gestational age
* No products expelled
* Fetal cardiac activity present on ultrasonography

---

# Investigations

### Blood

* Complete blood count
* Blood grouping and Rh typing
* Blood sugar
* Thyroid profile (if indicated)

### Urine

* Routine examination
* Pregnancy test

### Imaging

**Ultrasonography (Gold Standard)**

Shows

* Live intrauterine pregnancy
* Fetal cardiac activity
* Gestational sac
* Subchorionic bleed if present

### Others

* Serum β-hCG (serial measurements when diagnosis is uncertain)

---

# Management of Threatened Abortion

## 1. Admission (if indicated)

Indications

* Heavy bleeding
* Severe pain
* Hemodynamic instability
* Doubtful diagnosis

Otherwise, outpatient management is appropriate.

---

## 2. General Measures

* Reassure the patient
* Explain prognosis
* Advise adequate rest (strict bed rest is **not routinely recommended**)
* Avoid strenuous physical activity
* Avoid sexual intercourse until bleeding stops
* Maintain hydration

---

## 3. Medical Treatment

### Progesterone therapy

Useful in selected women, especially those with recurrent miscarriage or documented progesterone deficiency.

Examples

* Micronized progesterone
* Dydrogesterone

---

### Folic acid supplementation

* 400 µg/day

---

### Analgesics

* Paracetamol if pain is present

Avoid NSAIDs unless indicated.

---

### Anti-D Immunoglobulin

Indicated in **Rh-negative unsensitized women** with vaginal bleeding.

---

## 4. Follow-up

* Repeat ultrasonography after 1–2 weeks if necessary
* Monitor fetal viability
* Regular antenatal visits
* Advise to report immediately if:

  * Heavy bleeding
  * Severe abdominal pain
  * Passage of tissue
  * Fever

---

# Complications

* Progression to inevitable abortion
* Incomplete abortion
* Complete abortion
* Missed abortion
* Pregnancy loss

---

# Prognosis

If fetal cardiac activity is present and bleeding is mild, approximately **85–90%** of pregnancies continue successfully.

---

# Key Points for Exam

* Vaginal bleeding + **closed os** + **live fetus** = Threatened abortion.
* **Ultrasonography** is the investigation of choice.
* **Progesterone** may be beneficial in selected patients.
* Give **Anti-D** to Rh-negative women.
* Most pregnancies continue normally with appropriate management.

---

# Flowchart (Management)

```text
Threatened Abortion
        │
        ▼

        │
        ▼
Ultrasonography
        │
        ▼

        │
        ▼
```

# **Q.2. Methods to diagnose pregnancy and discuss the physiological changes in pregnancy.**

**References:** *DC Dutta's Textbook of Obstetrics (9th Ed.), Williams Obstetrics (26th Ed.)*

---

# Diagnosis of Pregnancy

Pregnancy is diagnosed based on **presumptive, probable, and positive (confirmatory) signs**.

## Classification of Signs of Pregnancy

| **Presumptive Signs** (Subjective)                      | **Probable Signs** (Objective) | **Positive Signs** (Diagnostic)         |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Amenorrhoea                                             | Positive urine pregnancy test  | Fetal heart sounds                      |
| Morning sickness                                        | Uterine enlargement            | Fetal movements felt by examiner        |
| Breast tenderness                                       | Hegar's sign                   | Ultrasonographic visualization of fetus |
| Frequency of micturition                                | Goodell's sign                 | Cardiac activity on USG                 |
| Fatigue                                                 | Chadwick's sign                |                                         |
| Quickening (18–20 weeks in primi, 16–18 weeks in multi) | Braxton-Hicks contractions     |                                         |

---

# Methods of Diagnosis

## 1. History

* Amenorrhoea (most important symptom)
* Nausea and vomiting
* Breast enlargement
* Frequency of urination
* Fatigue
* Quickening

---

## 2. Physical Examination

### General Examination

* Breast enlargement
* Hyperpigmentation of areola
* Montgomery tubercles
* Colostrum

### Abdominal Examination

* Progressive uterine enlargement
* Fundal height corresponding to gestational age
* Fetal movements
* Fetal parts palpable after 24 weeks

### Pelvic Examination

* Chadwick's sign (bluish discoloration of cervix)
* Goodell's sign (soft cervix)
* Hegar's sign (softening of uterine isthmus)
* Enlarged uterus

---

## 3. Laboratory Diagnosis

### Urine Pregnancy Test (UPT)

* Detects **β-hCG**
* Positive after missed period (4–5 weeks)
* Easy, rapid and inexpensive

### Serum β-hCG

* More sensitive than urine test
* Detectable **8–10 days after conception**
* Useful in:

  * Early pregnancy
  * Ectopic pregnancy
  * Molar pregnancy

---

## 4. Ultrasonography (Gold Standard)

### Transvaginal USG (TVS)

| Finding          | Gestational Age |
| ---------------- | --------------- |
| Gestational sac  | 4½–5 weeks      |
| Yolk sac         | 5 weeks         |
| Fetal pole       | 5½–6 weeks      |
| Cardiac activity | 6 weeks         |

### Transabdominal USG

* Confirms fetal viability
* Number of fetuses
* Placental location
* Gestational age
* Congenital anomalies

---

```text
Amenorrhoea
      ↓
History + Examination
      ↓
Urine Pregnancy Test
      ↓
Positive
      ↓
Ultrasonography
      ↓
Confirmation of Pregnancy
```

# Physiological Changes in Pregnancy

Pregnancy causes physiological adaptations in almost every organ system.

---

# 1. Reproductive System

### Uterus

* Weight increases from **50–70 g to 900–1000 g**
* Capacity increases from **10 mL to 5 L or more**
* Increased uterine blood flow

### Cervix

* Softening (Goodell's sign)
* Bluish discoloration (Chadwick's sign)
* Increased mucus secretion → mucus plug formation

### Vagina

* Increased vascularity
* Increased glycogen
* Acidic pH due to lactobacilli

---

# 2. Cardiovascular System

* Cardiac output ↑ by **30–50%**
* Blood volume ↑ by **40–50%**
* Heart rate ↑ by **10–15 beats/min**
* Mild fall in BP during second trimester
* Physiological systolic flow murmur may be heard

---

# 3. Hematological Changes

* Plasma volume ↑ more than RBC mass
* **Physiological anemia of pregnancy**
* Mild leukocytosis
* Hypercoagulable state
* ESR increased

---

# 4. Respiratory System

* Tidal volume ↑
* Minute ventilation ↑
* Mild respiratory alkalosis
* Functional residual capacity ↓
* Diaphragm elevated

---

# 5. Renal System

* Renal blood flow ↑
* GFR ↑ by approximately 50%
* Mild glycosuria may occur
* Frequency of micturition
* Dilatation of ureters (physiological hydroureter)

---

# 6. Gastrointestinal System

* Nausea and vomiting
* Gastric emptying delayed
* Heartburn (GERD)
* Constipation
* Gallbladder stasis

---

# 7. Endocrine Changes

* Increased estrogen
* Increased progesterone
* Increased hCG
* Increased prolactin
* Increased relaxin

---

# 8. Breast Changes

* Enlargement
* Increased vascularity
* Montgomery tubercles
* Colostrum secretion after 16 weeks

---

# 9. Skin Changes

* Linea nigra
* Chloasma gravidarum (mask of pregnancy)
* Striae gravidarum
* Hyperpigmentation

---

# 10. Musculoskeletal System

* Ligament laxity due to relaxin
* Lumbar lordosis
* Waddling gait
* Backache

---

# Summary Table

| System          | Physiological Changes                       |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| CVS             | ↑ Cardiac output, ↑ Blood volume            |
| Respiratory     | ↑ Minute ventilation                        |
| Renal           | ↑ GFR, frequency of micturition             |
| GIT             | Nausea, vomiting, constipation              |
| Hematology      | Physiological anemia, hypercoagulable state |
| Endocrine       | ↑ Estrogen, progesterone, hCG               |
| Skin            | Chloasma, linea nigra, striae               |
| Breast          | Enlargement, colostrum                      |
| Musculoskeletal | Lordosis, ligament laxity                   |

---

# Conclusion

Pregnancy is diagnosed by **history, clinical examination, pregnancy tests, and ultrasonography**, with **ultrasonography being the gold standard** for confirmation. Physiological changes occur in almost every organ system to support fetal growth and prepare the mother for labour and lactation.

# **Q.3. Discuss the pre-conceptional counselling, antenatal examination and obstetric examination.**

**References:** *DC Dutta's Textbook of Obstetrics, Williams Obstetrics, FOGSI Guidelines*

---

# Introduction

**Pre-conceptional counselling** aims to optimize maternal health before conception, while **antenatal and obstetric examination** help monitor maternal and fetal well-being throughout pregnancy for early detection of complications.

---

# I. Pre-conceptional Counselling

## Definition

Pre-conceptional counselling is the **systematic assessment and advice given to a woman and her partner before conception to improve pregnancy outcomes.**

---

## Objectives

* Ensure healthy mother and fetus
* Reduce maternal and fetal morbidity
* Detect and treat medical disorders
* Prevent congenital anomalies

---

## Components of Pre-conceptional Counselling

### 1. Detailed History

* Age
* Menstrual history
* Obstetric history
* Medical illnesses (DM, HTN, epilepsy, thyroid disorders)
* Surgical history
* Drug history
* Family history of genetic disorders
* Lifestyle habits (smoking, alcohol, substance abuse)

---

### 2. Physical Examination

* Height and weight
* BMI
* Blood pressure
* General systemic examination
* Pelvic examination if indicated

---

### 3. Laboratory Investigations

* Blood group and Rh typing
* CBC
* Blood sugar
* Thyroid function (if indicated)
* Urine routine examination
* HIV
* HBsAg
* VDRL
* Rubella immunity (if available)

---

### 4. Nutritional Advice

* Balanced diet
* Iron-rich foods
* Adequate protein intake
* Calcium supplementation
* Healthy weight maintenance

---

### 5. Folic Acid Supplementation

* **400 µg/day**
* Start **at least 1 month before conception**
* Continue through first trimester
* Prevents **Neural Tube Defects (NTDs)**

---

### 6. Vaccination

* Rubella (before pregnancy)
* Hepatitis B
* Varicella (if non-immune)
* COVID-19 (as per current recommendations)

---

### 7. Lifestyle Modification

* Stop smoking
* Avoid alcohol
* Avoid recreational drugs
* Regular exercise
* Stress reduction

---

### 8. Genetic Counselling

Indicated in:

* Previous child with congenital anomaly
* Recurrent abortions
* Consanguineous marriage
* Family history of inherited disorders

---

# II. Antenatal Examination

## Definition

Antenatal examination is the **systematic assessment of the pregnant woman during antenatal visits to monitor maternal and fetal health.**

---

## Components

### A. History

* LMP
* EDD
* Present complaints
* Obstetric history
* Medical history
* Drug history
* Immunization
* Fetal movements

---

### B. General Examination

* Pulse
* Blood pressure
* Temperature
* Respiratory rate
* Height
* Weight
* Pallor
* Edema
* Icterus
* Cyanosis
* Thyroid examination
* Breast examination

---

### C. Systemic Examination

* Cardiovascular system
* Respiratory system
* Abdomen
* CNS (if required)

---

# III. Obstetric Examination

Obstetric examination is mainly **abdominal examination** and is performed after 20 weeks.

---

## 1. Inspection

* Size of abdomen
* Shape
* Striae gravidarum
* Linea nigra
* Umbilicus
* Surgical scars
* Fetal movements

---

## 2. Palpation

### Fundal Height

Measured in centimetres from symphysis pubis to fundus.

---

### Fundal Grip (1st Leopold Maneuver)

Determines:

* Pole occupying fundus

---

### Lateral Grip (2nd Maneuver)

Determines:

* Position of fetal back

---

### Pawlik's Grip (3rd Maneuver)

Determines:

* Presenting part

---

### Pelvic Grip (4th Maneuver)

Determines:

* Engagement of presenting part

---

## 3. Auscultation

* Fetal Heart Sound (FHS)
* Normal: **110–160 beats/min**

---

## 4. Investigations During Antenatal Care

* CBC
* Blood group & Rh
* Urine routine and albumin/sugar
* HIV
* HBsAg
* VDRL
* Blood sugar
* Ultrasonography
* Anomaly scan
* Growth scans (when indicated)

---

# Antenatal Schedule (Brief)

* Up to 28 weeks → Every 4 weeks
* 28–36 weeks → Every 2 weeks
* After 36 weeks → Weekly until delivery

---

# Health Education

* Diet and nutrition
* Iron and calcium supplementation
* TT/Td immunization
* Warning signs (bleeding, leaking, reduced fetal movements, severe headache, swelling)
* Institutional delivery
* Breastfeeding counselling
* Birth preparedness

---

# Conclusion

Effective **pre-conceptional counselling**, thorough **antenatal examination**, and proper **obstetric examination** are essential to ensure early detection of high-risk pregnancies, reduce maternal and fetal complications, and improve pregnancy outcomes. These form the cornerstone of quality obstetric care and are frequently tested in university examinations.

# **SAQ 1. Diagnosis of Vesicular Mole (Hydatidiform Mole)**

## Definition

A **vesicular (hydatidiform) mole** is a form of **Gestational Trophoblastic Disease (GTD)** characterized by abnormal proliferation of trophoblastic tissue with swollen, edematous chorionic villi.

---

## Clinical Features

* Amenorrhoea
* Vaginal bleeding (most common symptom)
* Excessive nausea and vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum)
* Passage of grape-like vesicles
* Uterus larger than period of gestation
* Absence of fetal heart sounds
* Early-onset pre-eclampsia (<20 weeks)
* Symptoms of hyperthyroidism (occasionally)

---

## Investigations

### 1. Serum β-hCG

* Markedly elevated (much higher than expected for gestational age).

### 2. Ultrasonography (Investigation of Choice)

Shows:

* **"Snowstorm appearance"**
* Multiple cystic spaces
* No fetus in complete mole

### 3. Blood Tests

* CBC
* Blood group and Rh typing
* Liver and renal function tests
* Thyroid function tests (if indicated)

### 4. Chest X-ray

* To detect pulmonary metastasis if GTN is suspected.

---

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis is based on:

* Clinical features
* Very high β-hCG
* Characteristic USG findings
* Histopathological examination after evacuation (confirmation)

---

## Key Points

* **Investigation of choice:** Ultrasonography
* **Diagnostic marker:** Serum β-hCG
* **Classic USG finding:** Snowstorm appearance

---

# **SAQ 2. Hyperemesis Gravidarum**

## Definition

Hyperemesis gravidarum is **severe, persistent vomiting during pregnancy leading to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, ketonuria, and weight loss (>5% of pre-pregnancy weight).**

---

## Clinical Features

* Persistent vomiting
* Weight loss
* Dehydration
* Dry tongue
* Tachycardia
* Postural hypotension
* Oliguria
* Ketonuria

---

## Investigations

* CBC
* Urine ketones
* Serum electrolytes
* LFT
* RFT
* Thyroid function tests (if indicated)
* Ultrasonography (to exclude molar pregnancy or multiple pregnancy)

---

## Management

### General

* Hospitalization if severe
* IV fluids (Normal saline/Ringer lactate)
* Electrolyte correction
* Monitor urine output

### Drugs

* Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
* Doxylamine
* Metoclopramide
* Ondansetron (if needed)

### Nutritional Support

* Small frequent meals
* High-carbohydrate diet
* Vitamin supplementation

### Complications

* Dehydration
* Electrolyte imbalance
* Wernicke's encephalopathy
* Fetal growth restriction (rare)

---

# **SAQ 3. Management of Preterm Labour**

## Definition

Preterm labour is **labour occurring after 28 weeks and before 37 completed weeks of gestation**, with regular uterine contractions and cervical changes.

---

## Management

### 1. Admission and Assessment

* Confirm gestational age
* Assess maternal and fetal condition
* Rule out contraindications to tocolysis

---

### 2. Investigations

* CBC
* Urine examination
* CRP (if infection suspected)
* Ultrasound
* CTG/NST for fetal well-being

---

### 3. Corticosteroids (24–34 weeks)

To accelerate fetal lung maturity.

* Betamethasone **12 mg IM**, two doses 24 hours apart

**OR**

* Dexamethasone **6 mg IM every 12 hours × 4 doses**

---

### 4. Tocolytics

Used for 48 hours to allow steroid action.

Examples:

* Nifedipine (Drug of choice)
* Terbutaline
* Indomethacin (selected cases)
* Atosiban (where available)

---

### 5. Magnesium Sulphate

If gestation <32 weeks:

* Neuroprotection against cerebral palsy.

---

### 6. Antibiotics

Only if:

* PPROM
* Group B Streptococcus prophylaxis
* Documented infection

---

### 7. Delivery

Indications:

* Fetal distress
* Chorioamnionitis
* Severe maternal disease
* Failed tocolysis

---

# **SAQ 4. Discuss the Various Causes of Recurrent Pregnancy Loss**

## Definition

Recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL) is defined as **two or more consecutive pregnancy losses before fetal viability** (current accepted definition by many societies; older texts may mention three).

---

## Causes

### 1. Genetic

* Parental balanced translocation
* Chromosomal abnormalities

### 2. Anatomical

* Septate uterus
* Bicornuate uterus
* Cervical incompetence
* Fibroids
* Intrauterine adhesions

### 3. Endocrine

* Diabetes mellitus
* Hypothyroidism
* PCOS
* Luteal phase defect

### 4. Immunological

* Antiphospholipid syndrome
* Autoimmune disorders

### 5. Infectious

* TORCH infections (less common cause)
* Chronic endometritis

### 6. Thrombophilia

* Factor V Leiden mutation
* Protein C deficiency
* Protein S deficiency

### 7. Lifestyle Factors

* Smoking
* Alcohol
* Obesity
* Excess caffeine

### 8. Unexplained

* Approximately 40–50% cases

# **SAQ 5. Ectopic Pregnancy – Definition, Sites and Management**

## Definition

Ectopic pregnancy is **implantation of the fertilized ovum outside the uterine cavity.**

---

## Sites

### Tubal (95%)

* Ampulla (most common)
* Isthmus
* Fimbria
* Interstitial

### Non-Tubal

* Ovary
* Cervix
* Abdominal cavity
* Caesarean scar pregnancy

---

## Clinical Features

* Amenorrhoea
* Lower abdominal pain
* Vaginal bleeding
* Syncope (if ruptured)

---

## Investigations

* Urine pregnancy test
* Serum β-hCG
* Transvaginal ultrasound (investigation of choice)
* CBC
* Blood grouping and Rh typing

---

## Management

### Medical

**Methotrexate** (single-dose protocol)

Indications:

* Hemodynamically stable
* Unruptured ectopic
* Small sac (<3.5–4 cm)
* No fetal cardiac activity (in most cases)
* Appropriate β-hCG levels

---

### Surgical

* Laparoscopic salpingostomy
* Salpingectomy (most common)
* Emergency laparotomy in ruptured ectopic

---

### Rh-negative Women

* Administer Anti-D immunoglobulin.

# **AETCOM (5 Marks)**

## **Question:** *A 25-year-old primigravida, unbooked and unregistered, delivers a stillborn baby. The relatives are waiting anxiously outside the labour room. As the treating doctor, how will you break the news to the family?*

### Answer (Using the SPIKES Protocol)

### 1. **S – Setting**

* Meet the family in a private, quiet room.
* Ensure there are no interruptions.
* Sit at eye level and maintain a calm demeanor.

### 2. **P – Perception**

* Ask what they already understand about the mother's and baby's condition.
* Assess their expectations and emotional state.

### 3. **I – Invitation**

* Ask if they are ready to discuss the outcome.
* Confirm that the closest family members are present if the patient wishes.

### 4. **K – Knowledge**

* Deliver the news gently and clearly.
* Use simple language:

  > "I'm very sorry to tell you that despite all our efforts, your baby could not be saved."
* Avoid medical jargon and avoid giving false reassurance.

### 5. **E – Empathy**

* Allow silence and time for the family to react.
* Acknowledge their grief:

  * "I can see how painful this is."
  * "I am deeply sorry for your loss."

### 6. **S – Strategy and Support**

* Explain the likely cause if known, or state that further evaluation may be needed.
* Reassure them that the mother is receiving appropriate care.
* Discuss the next steps, including investigations, emotional support, and follow-up.
* Offer bereavement counseling if available.

### **Key Ethical Principles**

* Compassion and empathy.
* Honest, clear communication.
* Respect for privacy and confidentiality.
* Answer questions patiently and truthfully.
* Avoid blaming the patient or family.
