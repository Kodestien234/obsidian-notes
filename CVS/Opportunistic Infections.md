# Fungal Infections: Opportunistic and Systemic Mycoses

## 1. Candidiasis (Opportunistic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Pathogenesis (Summarized)
*   **Etiology:** *Candida* species are yeast-like fungi that produce pseudohyphae [1]. *C. albicans* is the most pathogenic, but non-albicans species include *C. tropicalis*, *C. glabrata*, *C. krusei*, and the highly multidrug-resistant nosocomial *C. auris* [1, 2].
*   **Predisposing Factors:** Extremes of age, low immunity (HIV, steroids, malignancies), broad-spectrum antibiotics, and diabetes mellitus [3].
*   **Virulence Factors:** Adhesins (for attachment), Enzymes (aspartyl and serine proteinases), Glycoprotein toxins, and Pseudohyphae [3].
*   **Clinical:** Mucosal (oral thrush, vulvovaginitis), Cutaneous (intertrigo, paronychia), and Invasive candidiasis (septicemia, meningitis, endophthalmitis) [3].

### B. Laboratory Diagnosis (Exhaustive)
*   **Specimens:** Urine, blood, mucosal swabs, or skin scrapings. Blood can be inoculated into standard culture bottles like BacT/ALERT [4].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy:** Gram stain shows Gram-positive oval budding yeast cells with characteristic pseudohyphae [3, 4].
*   **2. Culture:** Inoculation on Sabouraud Dextrose Agar (SDA) yields creamy white and pasty colonies [3, 4].
*   **3. Species Identification Tests:**
    *   **Germ Tube Test:** Colonies are mixed with human or sheep serum and incubated for 2 hours; a wet mount shows long tube-like projections extending from the yeast cells (Positive for *C. albicans*) [4].
    *   **Dalmau Plate Culture:** Used to demonstrate chlamydospore production (Positive for *C. albicans*) [4].
    *   **CHROMagar:** Differentiates species by producing different colored colonies [4].
    *   **Growth at 45°C:** Positive specifically for *C. albicans* [4].
    *   **Biochemicals:** Carbohydrate assimilation and fermentation tests [4].
    *   **Automated Systems:** MALDI-TOF and VITEK (Especially crucial for identifying *C. auris*) [2, 4].
    *   **Molecular Methods:** PCR detecting specific genes [4].
*   **4. Immunodiagnosis:**
    *   **Antibody/Antigen Detection:** ELISA against cell wall mannan antigen or cytoplasmic antigen [4].
    *   **Enzyme Detection:** Enolase, aspartate proteinase [4].
    *   **Metabolite Detection:** Mannitol and arabinitol [4].
*   **5. $\beta$-d-Glucan Assay:** A highly specific marker for invasive fungal infections, detected in blood via colorimetric enzyme immunoassay (used for diagnosis and monitoring treatment) [2, 4].

### C. Treatment
*   Topical azoles for cutaneous lesions; Oral fluconazole, voriconazole, caspofungin, or Amphotericin B for systemic infections [2].

## 2. Cryptococcosis (Opportunistic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Pathogenesis (Summarized)
*   **Etiology:** *Cryptococcus neoformans* (serotypes A/D, found in pigeon droppings) and *C. gattii* (serotypes B/C, found in eucalyptus trees) are capsulated yeasts [5, 6].
*   **Pathogenesis:** Acquired by inhalation $\rightarrow$ infects lungs $\rightarrow$ disseminates via blood to the CNS [5, 6]. 
*   **Virulence:** Polysaccharide capsule (anti-phagocytic) and Melanin production (via phenyl oxidase) [6].
*   **Clinical Manifestations:** Pulmonary cryptococcosis and Chronic Cryptococcal Meningitis (headache, memory loss, cranial nerve paresis) [6].

### B. Laboratory Diagnosis (Exhaustive)
*   **Specimens:** CSF, blood, or skin scrapings [6].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy & Staining:**
    *   **Negative Staining (Modified India Ink):** Demonstrates the capsule as a refractile delineated clear space surrounding round budding yeast cells against a black background (60–70% sensitive) [6].
    *   **Gram Stain:** Shows Gram-positive round budding yeast cells [6].
    *   **Mucicarmine Stain:** Specifically stains the carminophilic cell wall [6].
    *   **Masson-Fontana Stain:** Demonstrates melanin production [6].
    *   **Alcian Blue Stain:** Used to demonstrate the capsule [6].
*   **2. Antigen Detection:** Capsular antigens are detected from CSF or serum via the Latex Agglutination test (rapid, sensitive, specific) [6].
*   **3. Culture:** CSF is inoculated onto SDA, blood agar, or chocolate agar at 37°C. Colonies appear mucoid, creamy white, and yeast-like [7].
*   **4. Confirmation & Species ID:**
    *   **Niger Seed / Bird Seed Agar:** Demonstrates melanin production, yielding brown-colored colonies [7].
    *   **Growth at 37°C** [7].
    *   **Urease Test:** Positive [7].
    *   **Assimilation:** Inositol and nitrate assimilation tests [7].
    *   **Pathogenicity:** Mouse pathogenicity test [7].
    *   **Automated Systems:** MALDI-TOF [7].

### C. Treatment
*   Fluconazole (without CNS involvement) or Induction with Amphotericin B ± flucytosine (with CNS involvement) [7, 8].

## 3. Histoplasmosis (Systemic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Pathogenesis (Summarized)
*   **Etiology:** Darling's disease is caused by *Histoplasma capsulatum*, a dimorphic fungus [9].
*   **Pathogenesis:** Inhalation of microconidia from soil contaminated with bird/bat droppings $\rightarrow$ engulfed by alveolar macrophages $\rightarrow$ transform into yeast forms surviving inside the phagolysosome $\rightarrow$ disseminate via blood [9].
*   **Clinical:** Acute/chronic pulmonary histoplasmosis, and disseminated forms affecting bone marrow, spleen, and liver in HIV patients [9, 10].

### B. Laboratory Diagnosis (Exhaustive)
*   **Specimens:** Sputum, bone marrow aspirate, blood [10].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy (Histopathology):** Staining of specimens reveals tiny oval yeast cells (2–4 µm in size) with narrow-based budding, typically located intracellularly [10].
*   **2. Culture (Demonstration of Dimorphism):**
    *   **At 25°C on SDA:** Yields the mycelial (mold) form, classically described as having **tuberculate macroconidia** [10].
    *   **At 37°C on SDA:** Yields the yeast form (creamy white colonies) [10].
*   **3. Skin Test:** Uses histoplasmin antigen to demonstrate a delayed-type hypersensitivity response (indicating prior exposure) [10].
*   **4. Molecular Methods:** PCR targeting the specific ITS D1/D2 gene [10].

### C. Treatment
*   Liposomal Amphotericin B [10].

## 4. Coccidioidomycosis (Systemic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Pathogenesis (Summarized)
*   **Etiology:** Valley fever / desert rheumatism is caused by the dimorphic fungus *Coccidioides immitis* [11].
*   **Pathogenesis:** Inhalation of arthroconidia $\rightarrow$ enlarge in the lungs $\rightarrow$ develop internal septations to form massive sac-like structures (up to 200 µm) called **spherules**, which encompass numerous endospores [11].

### B. Laboratory Diagnosis (Exhaustive)
*   **Specimens:** Sputum or tissue biopsy [11].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy (Histopathology):** Staining of sputum/tissue biopsy specimens demonstrates **spherules** which are large sac-like structures (20–80 µm in size) completely filled with endospores [11].
*   **2. Culture (Demonstration of Dimorphism):** Cultures on SDA produce mycelial growth, characteristically described as fragmented hyphae consisting of **barrel-shaped arthrospores** with alternate distorted/empty cells [11, 12].
*   **3. Serology:** Antibodies are detected by the immunodiffusion test [12].
*   **4. Skin Test:** Uses fungal extracts (coccidioidin or spherulin). A reaction of $\ge$ 5 mm induration within 48 hours indicates a delayed hypersensitivity reaction from past infection [12].

### C. Treatment
*   Azoles such as Itraconazole [12].
