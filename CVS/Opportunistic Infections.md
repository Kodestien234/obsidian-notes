# Fungal Infections: Opportunistic and Systemic Mycoses

*(Exam Note: Fungal infections are broadly categorized. *Candida* and *Cryptococcus* are primarily opportunistic fungi infecting immunocompromised hosts, while *Histoplasma* and *Coccidioides* are dimorphic fungi causing systemic (deep) mycoses [1, 2].)*

---

## 1. Candidiasis (Opportunistic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Etiological Agent
*   **Definition:** The most common fungal disease in humans affecting skin, mucosa, and internal organs [3]. 
*   **Causative Agent:** *Candida* species, a yeast-like fungus that produces pseudohyphae [3].
*   **Important Species:** *C. albicans* (most pathogenic), *C. tropicalis*, *C. glabrata*, *C. krusei*, and the highly drug-resistant nosocomial *C. auris* [3, 4].

### B. Pathogenesis & Risk Factors
*   **Predisposing Factors:** Extremes of age, pregnancy, HIV infection, prolonged broad-spectrum antibiotics, steroids, immunosuppressive drugs, and diabetes mellitus [5].
*   **Virulence Factors:** 
    *   **Adhesins:** For epithelial attachment [5].
    *   **Enzymes:** ==Aspartyl proteinases== and ==serine proteinases== for tissue invasion [5].
    *   **Pseudohyphae:** Indicates active infection and aids in tissue invasion [5].

### C. Clinical Manifestations
*   **Mucosal:** Oral thrush, vulvovaginitis, esophageal candidiasis [5].
*   **Cutaneous:** Intertrigo (skin folds), paronychia, onychomycosis [5].
*   **Invasive:** UTI, pulmonary candidiasis, septicemia, meningitis, and endophthalmitis [5].

### D. Laboratory Diagnosis (Most Important Section)
*   **Specimen:** Urine, blood, mucosal swabs, or skin scrapings depending on the site [6].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy:** Gram stain shows Gram-positive oval budding yeast cells with characteristic pseudohyphae [5, 6].
*   **2. Culture:** Inoculation on Sabouraud Dextrose Agar (SDA) yields creamy white and pasty colonies [5, 6].
*   **3. Species Identification Tests:**
    *   **Germ Tube Test (Reynolds Braudes phenomenon):** Colonies mixed with human/sheep serum and incubated for 2 hours. Formation of long tube-like projections is diagnostic and positive for *C. albicans* [6].
    *   **Dalmau Plate Culture:** Used to demonstrate chlamydospore production (positive for *C. albicans*) [6].
    *   **CHROMagar:** Differentiates species by producing different colored colonies [6].
    *   **Growth at 45°C:** Positive for *C. albicans* [6].
    *   **Biochemicals:** Carbohydrate assimilation and fermentation tests [6].
    *   **Automated Systems:** MALDI-TOF and VITEK [6].
*   **4. Immunodiagnosis:** Detection of cell wall mannan antigen or cytoplasmic antigen via ELISA [6].
*   **5. Molecular Methods:** PCR targeting species-specific genes [6].
*   **6. $\beta$-d-Glucan Assay:** A highly specific marker for invasive fungal infections, detected in blood via colorimetric enzyme immunoassay (used for diagnosis and monitoring treatment) [4, 6].

### E. Treatment
*   **Cutaneous:** Topical azoles [4].
*   **Systemic/Invasive:** Oral fluconazole, voriconazole, caspofungin, or Amphotericin B [4].

## 2. Cryptococcosis (Opportunistic Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Etiological Agent
*   **Causative Agent:** *Cryptococcus neoformans*, a capsulated yeast [1].
*   **Serotypes:** *C. neoformans* correlates with serotypes A and D (found worldwide in pigeon droppings), while *C. gattii* correlates with serotypes B and C (found in eucalyptus trees) [1, 7].

### B. Pathogenesis & Risk Factors
*   **Transmission:** Acquired by inhalation of yeast cells from soil contaminated with avian/pigeon excreta [1, 7].
*   **Spread:** Infects lungs first, then disseminates via blood primarily to the CNS (neurotropic) [7].
*   **Virulence Factors:** Polysaccharide capsule (principal anti-phagocytic factor) and Melanin production (via phenyl oxidase) [7].
*   **Risk Groups:** Advanced HIV/AIDS patients, hematologic malignancies, and transplant recipients [7].

### C. Clinical Manifestations
*   **Pulmonary Cryptococcosis:** First and most common presentation [7].
*   **Cryptococcal Meningitis:** Chronic meningitis with fever, headache, memory loss, and loss of vision [7].

### D. Laboratory Diagnosis (Most Important Section)
*   **Specimen:** CSF, blood, or skin scrapings [7].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy & Staining:**
    *   **Negative Staining (Modified India Ink):** Demonstrates the capsule as a clear, refractile delineated space surrounding round budding yeast cells against a black background [7].
    *   **Gram Stain:** Shows Gram-positive round budding yeast cells [7].
    *   **Mucicarmine Stain:** Specifically stains the carminophilic cell wall [7].
    *   **Masson-Fontana Stain:** Demonstrates melanin production [7].
    *   **Alcian Blue Stain:** Demonstrates the capsule [7].
*   **2. Antigen Detection:** Rapid detection of capsular antigens in CSF or serum via Latex Agglutination test (highly sensitive and specific) [7].
*   **3. Culture:** Inoculated on SDA, Blood agar, or Chocolate agar at 37°C. Colonies appear as mucoid, creamy white, and yeast-like [8].
*   **4. Confirmation & Species ID:**
    *   **Niger Seed Agar / Bird Seed Agar:** Shows brown-colored colonies (demonstrates melanin production) [8].
    *   **Urease Test:** Positive [8].
    *   **Growth at 37°C** and assimilation of inositol and nitrate [8].

### E. Treatment
*   **Without CNS involvement:** Fluconazole [8].
*   **With CNS involvement:** Induction with Amphotericin B ± flucytosine, followed by oral fluconazole [9].

## 3. Histoplasmosis (Systemic / Deep Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Etiological Agent
*   **Definition:** Also known as Darling's disease, it is a systemic granulomatous disease [2].
*   **Causative Agent:** *Histoplasma capsulatum*, a dimorphic fungus [2]. (Exists as a yeast at 37°C in humans, and as a mold at 25°C in the environment) [2].

### B. Pathogenesis
*   **Transmission:** Inhalation of microconidia (spores) from soil contaminated with bird or bat droppings [2].
*   **Mechanism:** Spores enter lungs, are engulfed by alveolar macrophages, and transform into yeast forms. They survive inside the phagolysosome and disseminate via the bloodstream [2].

### C. Clinical Manifestations
*   **Pulmonary:** Acute flu-like illness or chronic cavitary histoplasmosis [2, 10].
*   **Disseminated:** Targets the reticuloendothelial system (bone marrow, spleen, liver) especially in HIV/AIDS patients [10].

### D. Laboratory Diagnosis (Most Important Section)
*   **Specimen:** Sputum, bone marrow aspirate, blood [10].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy (Histopathology):** Giemsa staining reveals tiny, oval yeast cells (2–4 µm) with narrow-based budding, typically located intracellularly inside macrophages [10, 11].
*   **2. Culture (Demonstration of Dimorphism):**
    *   **At 25°C on SDA:** Yields the mycelial (mold) form, classically described as septate hyphae with **tuberculate macroconidia** [10].
    *   **At 37°C on SDA:** Yields the yeast form (creamy white colonies) [10].
*   **3. Immunodiagnosis (Skin Test):** Histoplasmin antigen injected intradermally demonstrates delayed-type hypersensitivity (indicates prior exposure) [10].
*   **4. Molecular Methods:** PCR targeting the specific ITS D1/D2 gene [10].

### E. Treatment
*   Liposomal Amphotericin B is the drug of choice [10].

## 4. Coccidioidomycosis (Systemic / Deep Mycosis)

### A. Introduction & Etiological Agent
*   **Definition:** Also known as Valley fever or desert rheumatism [12].
*   **Causative Agent:** *Coccidioides immitis*, a dimorphic fungus [2, 12].

### B. Pathogenesis & Clinical Manifestations
*   **Transmission:** Acquired via inhalation of arthroconidia from the environment [12].
*   **Mechanism:** In the lungs, they enlarge, become rounded, and develop internal septations to form massive sac-like structures called **spherules** (up to 200 µm) filled with numerous endospores [12].
*   **Clinical:** Primarily pulmonary infection, but can disseminate to skin, bone, joints, and meninges [12].

### C. Laboratory Diagnosis (Most Important Section)
*   **Specimen:** Sputum or tissue biopsy [12].
*   **1. Direct Microscopy (Histopathology):** Staining (e.g., PAS stain) demonstrates large sac-like **spherules** (20–80 µm in size) completely filled with endospores [12, 13].
*   **2. Culture:** SDA produces mycelial growth that is highly characteristic: fragmented hyphae consisting of **barrel-shaped arthrospores** separated by alternate distorted/empty cells [12, 13].
*   **3. Serology:** Antibodies are detected using the immunodiffusion test [13].
*   **4. Skin Test:** Injection of fungal extracts (coccidioidin or spherulin) produces $\ge$ 5 mm induration within 48 hours, indicating delayed hypersensitivity from past infection [13].

### D. Treatment
*   Azoles (e.g., Itraconazole) are the drugs of choice [13].
